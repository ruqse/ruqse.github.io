About me

As a PhD student in Biomedical sciences, I'm always looking for new ways to use computer programs and wet-lab techniques to solve biological and medical problems. I've dealt with a wide range of infectious enemies, including viruses, fungi, bacteria, and now parasites. These pesky critters have proven to be particularly tough, as they appear to be drug resistant. That's where I come in; I'm determined to figure out why and come up with a solution. So, if you have a biological or medical mystery that needs to be solved, don't hesitate to reach out. I'm always ready to jump into the fray and do my best to help, all while cracking a few jokes along the way to bring a little bit of joy and levity to the situation.

Uncovering the Secrets of Ivermectin Resistance: Comparing Gene Expression in P. univalens and C. elegans -2022

In this study, we used RNA sequencing and bioinformatics analysis to evaluate the use of the model organism Caenorhabditis elegans as a tool for understanding drug metabolism and resistance in the parasitic nematode Parascaris univalens. P. univalens is a major pathogen that infects horses and causes significant morbidity, but the molecular mechanisms underlying drug activity and resistance in this parasite are not well understood due to the lack of experimental in vitro models. To address this, we exposed adult P. univalens worms to the anthelmintic drug ivermectin (IVM) and measured gene expression changes using RNA sequencing. We identified 1085 differentially expressed genes in P. univalens after IVM exposure and selected 15 of these genes for further study in IVM-exposed C. elegans. However, we found that there was only minor overlap in the gene expression changes induced by IVM in the two species. This is the first time that C. elegans has been used as a model for understanding drug metabolism and resistance in P. univalens, and while the approach appears promising, it was limited by the small number of genes studied. Future studies with a larger number of genes may be able to identify additional candidate genes involved in drug metabolism and resistance.

Uncovering the Link Between Hedgehogs and the Deadly Bacteria MRSA -2021

In this pilot study, we set out to understand the relationship between methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus with the mecC gene (mecC-MRSA) and dermatophytes in wild European hedgehogs. Previous research has suggested that hedgehogs may be a natural reservoir for mecC-MRSA, but the reason for this high carriage rate is not known. We hypothesized that mecC-MRSA may have evolved on the skin of hedgehogs due to the presence of antibiotic-producing dermatophytes. We collected samples from 23 hedgehogs and used selective cultivation methods to screen for the presence of dermatophytes and mecC-MRSA. We identified Trichophyton erinacei producing benzylpenicillin in two hedgehogs, and these same hedgehogs carried mecC-MRSA. Overall, 60% of the hedgehogs in our study carried mecC-MRSA. These findings provide evidence that T. erinacei isolated from Swedish hedgehogs can produce benzylpenicillin and that these benzylpenicillin-producing T. erinacei co-occur with mecC-MRSA. Our study also confirms the high occurrence of mecC-MRSA among hedgehogs

Worms Under the Influence: Deciphering the Transcriptional Changes of P. univalens After Drug Exposure -2020

In this study, we sought to understand the transcriptional changes in adult Parascaris univalens worms in response to exposure to three different anthelmintic drugs: ivermectin, pyrantel citrate, and thiabendazole. These drugs are commonly used to treat infections caused by P. univalens, but some worms have developed resistance to them. Using RNA sequencing and bioinformatics analyses we exposed adult worms collected from naturally infected foals to various concentrations of the drugs and measured the viability of the worms after incubation. We found that all worms were alive at the end of the incubation, but their viability varied depending on the drug and concentration used. Our analysis identified several genes that were differentially expressed in response to drug exposure, including members of the phase I metabolic pathway and several superfamily of transporters. This is the first time that the expression of these genes has been described in P. univalens, and further work should be done to understand their potential role in drug metabolism and anthelmintic resistance.
